The writer uses language to create an atmosphere of uncanny preservation and suspended time.

The description of the space being "carefully held in place" uses the verb "held," which suggests a deliberate, almost supernatural force maintaining the room’s stillness. This is reinforced by the paradox that although dust was "everywhere," it "did not suggest neglect." Usually, the noun "neglect" connotes abandonment and decay, but by rejecting it, the writer implies the library is a sacred or "preserved" environment where time has stopped rather than moved on.

Furthermore, the atmosphere is depicted as resistant to the outside world, as the writer notes that "change was something that happened reluctantly here." The personification of "change" acting "reluctantly" creates a sense of tension, suggesting the room has its own stubborn will to remain exactly as it is. This is further emphasized by the sensory detail of the "dry and faintly bitter" smell of paper and ink, which evokes a sense of age that is sharp and present rather than fading away. The atmosphere becomes overwhelming for Nia, as the writer notes her original purpose "no longer mattered," suggesting the heavy, timeless air of the library has completely consumed her focus.
