The text commences with an exposition of the library and it's surroundings, creating an atmosphere of seclusion as the library appeared to be detached from the street despite eminently being a part of it. Through the use of the third person omnipresent to present the library as a different world as "the noise from the street disappeared completely" when Nia entered, the author introduces the reader to the tranquil nature of the library and the comfort accompanied by it, hooking the reader subtly as Nia's seemingly saturnine temperament was almost non-existent when she entered, depicting the idea that despite the detached appearance of the library, the comfort it offered transcends beyond any other plausible delight, beckoning the reader to proceed further into the story.
   Midway throughout the text, the writer introduces  ideas of pre- ordained doom as Nia begins to feel watched despite being alone. The place she had found comfort, had suddenly evoked fears in her and had suddenly turned into a foe, displaying  hostility towards her. This suddenly plunges the reader into an uncomfortable position and puts questions into their head as they begin to wonder who was lingering around Nia and why they were there, creating an atmospheric hook and making the reader unable to abandon the plot, encouraging them to read further to unfold the tension.
