The first thing I noticed was the silence. 

It was the kind that pressed inwards, oppressive and sentient, as though the world itself had decided to hold its breath. I stood still, keys clenched tightly in my palm, listening. No voices. No engines. Not even the distant hum of electricity. Just the quiet - watching me. 

The streets looked ordinary enough. Rows of houses stood shoulder to shoulder, their windows dark, their curtains drawn like closed eyelids. A single street lamp flickered overhead, its light wavering as though uncertain of its own existence. I had walked this road a hundred times before, yet now it felt unfamiliar, subtly rearranged. 

Disquieting. 

I took a step forward. The sound of my foot against the pavement echoed louder that it should have, snapping the fragile calm. Somewhere nearby, something shifted. I couldn't see it, but I felt it - the sudden sensation of being observed, assessed. My reflection stared back at me from a parked car window: pale, rigid, already braced for something i couldn't yet name.  

As I Walked, memories resurfaced uninvited. The argument. The slammed door. The word I had meant to retract but never did. I dismissed them, telling myself fatigue had a way of distorting reality, of animating shadows. Still, my grip tightened around the keys, their serrated metal edges biting into my skin. 

Halfway down the street, I noticed the door. 

It stood ajar, just slightly, a thin incision of darkness cutting through the warm glow inside. The light spilled onto the pavement, too precise, too intentional. My house. I was certain I had locked it. I always locked it! 

I stopped 

Every instinct urged me to turn back, to retreat and leave the silence undisturbed, but my feet remained anchored to the ground. The quiet had altered now - no longer vacant but anticipatory. The air felt dense, as though the moment itself carried consequence. I inhaled and stepped closer. Inside, something moved. 

The door creaked softly, widening just enough to invite me in, and as the light shifted, I understood that whatever waited on the other side already knew I was there. 

I expected a thief, a murderer, a shadow of danger—but the shape that emerged made my stomach twist in ways fear had never prepared me for.

It was her.

My mother.

The years fell away, leaving only questions and the weight of unfinished words between us. Her eyes searched mine—not with anger, not with warmth—but with something I couldn’t name. And in that silence, the house seemed to hold its breath with me, waiting for what would happen next.
