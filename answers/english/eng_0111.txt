Before the storm arrived, the air changed. It thickened and grew heavy, pressing against the skin. The sharp tang of brine clung to every breath, mixed with the earthy scent of petrichor rising from the damp ground along the shore. The sea lay dark and tense, its surface tight and glossy, as though it were holding something back.

Long before the storm broke, the sea seemed to sense it. The water lay dark and heavy beneath the sky, its surface stretched tight like skin pulled over bone. The horizon had faded, erased by thickening cloud, and the world felt smaller, enclosed. The air pressed close, damp and sour, filled with the sharp tang of brine and the earthy scent of petrichor drifting in from the soaked shoreline. Each breath tasted metallic, as if the storm had already begun to rust the air itself.

Closer to the harbour, small movements betrayed the change. Ropes sagged under the weight of moisture, rough fibres swollen and gritty against the posts they clung to. The boats rocked in shallow, uneven motions, knocking gently against one another with a hollow thud that echoed across the water. Beneath them, the sea lapped at the stone walls, cold and deliberate, leaving thin trails of foam that hissed softly before dissolving back into the blackness.

The wind arrived quietly at first. It crept low across the surface, lifting it into tight ripples that shivered and spread. Spray rose into the air, fine and sharp, settling on skin and lips, leaving behind a crust of salt. The smell of wet timber deepened as water seeped into old wood, releasing the heavy scent of age and decay. Masts trembled, sails snapped once, then fell slack, and the boats shifted restlessly, as though trying to pull away from their moorings.

Then the storm found its voice. The wind surged forward, no longer cautious, tearing across the sea and ripping the waves apart. Rain fell in sudden sheets, thick and relentless, striking the water hard enough to burst and scatter. The sound grew deafening. Water crashed against hulls, ropes snapped tight, and the wind roared with a force that seemed to flatten everything beneath it. Waves rose higher now, swelling and folding over themselves, their tops shredded into white foam that burned against the dark water.

The sea became violent. It surged forward in heavy, muscular movements, throwing itself against the harbour walls and dragging back again, as though furious at being restrained. Boats pitched wildly, hulls slamming, wood groaning under the strain. The smell of salt was everywhere, sharp and choking, mixed with the sourness of soaked rope and the clean sting of rain.

Without warning, the sky split open. Lightning tore through the clouds, flooding the sea with harsh white light. For a single frozen moment, everything was revealed. The towering waves, the strained ropes, the boats thrown at impossible angles. Foam streaked across the water like torn fabric. Thunder followed instantly, deep and crushing, rolling across the sea and vibrating through bone and chest alike. Even after it faded, the air seemed to tremble.

Gradually, the storm began to weaken. The rain loosened its grip, falling in broken bursts instead of solid force. The wind faltered, stumbling into uneven gusts before retreating altogether. Waves still rolled across the sea, but their edges softened, their movements slower and heavier, as if exhausted by their own violence.

In the aftermath, silence returned carefully. The air smelled washed and clean now, stripped of metal and earth, leaving only salt and wet wood behind. The boats drifted gently, soaked and scarred, rocking in the uneasy calm. Water slid quietly along the harbour walls, no longer striking but whispering as it passed.

Above, the clouds thinned, pulling apart to reveal a pale and fragile light that touched the surface of the sea. The water lay dark and reflective once more, calm but watchful. Though the storm had passed, its presence lingered, held beneath the surface, waiting patiently to rise again.
