The Unexpected Visitor
The storm arrived without warning.
Rain hammered the windows in sudden, furious bursts, and the wind prowled around the house like something searching for a way in. Mrs Turner switched off the television because it seemed rude to speak over weather like this. The silence afterward felt huge — except for the steady ticking of the hallway clock and the restless sigh of the chimney.
Then came the knock.
It was not loud. In fact, it was so soft that she thought, at first, she must have imagined it. She waited, still and listening. Another knock followed — three faint taps, patient, like someone politely reminding the house that they were still there.
No one visited at this time of night.
She tightened her cardigan around herself and opened the door only a cautious inch. On the step stood a boy, perhaps twelve, soaked through, with dark hair plastered to his forehead and a backpack hanging limply from one shoulder. His eyes — startlingly calm — met hers.
“I’m sorry to bother you,” he said quietly. “My map’s wrong. I think I’m lost.”
There was something about his voice — gentle, careful, as though he had practised the sentence beforehand. The rain spilled off the gutter and splashed onto his shoes. She hesitated only a moment before stepping back.
“Come in before you disappear completely,” she said. “You’ll catch your death.”
He wiped his feet, which made her smile, and stood in the hallway as if afraid to disturb anything. Up close, he seemed even thinner, almost delicate, the way winter branches look when the leaves have gone. She made tea while he sat at the kitchen table, watching the steam curl upwards.
“What’s your name?” she asked.
He glanced at the window, as though checking the storm was still there. “Evan,” he said.
“And where are you going?”
He swallowed. “Home.”
She frowned. “Where’s that?”
He opened his mouth, then closed it again. Finally, he shrugged. “It used to be near here.”
The answer unsettled her. She pushed the mug toward him. His fingers trembled slightly when he lifted it, but he smiled with real gratitude.
They spoke in fragments. He had been walking a long time. He had lost the path. His phone battery had died. None of it felt exactly untrue, yet there were gaps — like a photograph with pieces quietly cut out. Still, something in her softened. She had once had a son who had gone walking and not come back. The thought brushed against her heart and retreated, as it always did.
“Stay a while,” she said. “The storm will pass.”
He nodded, relaxing a little, and looked around the kitchen as if memorising it. His gaze lingered on the family photographs along the shelf. One frame held the faded image of a boy at the seaside, hair windblown, eyes bright with mischief.
“That’s Daniel,” she said, before she could stop herself. “My son.”
Evan tilted his head. “He looks kind.”
“He was.” Her voice wavered, then steadied. “The sea took him, years ago.”
A silence unfolded, gentle rather than awkward. Evan placed his mug on the table and stood.
“Thank you for the tea,” he said. “I think I know the way now.”
“But the storm—”
“It’s easing.” He gave a small, uncertain smile, as though unsure whether smiles were still allowed. “You helped.”
She walked him to the door. When she opened it, she was startled to find the rain had thinned to a whisper. The sky was lighter, washed clean. He stepped onto the path, lifted his hand in a quiet farewell, and began to walk toward the bend in the lane.
“Evan!” she called. “Do you need a coat?”
He didn’t turn, but he lifted his hand again, as if the offer had reached him and warmed him anyway.
She closed the door and leaned against it, surprised by the ache of missing someone she had only just met. The house felt strangely different — not fuller, exactly, but softer, as though some long-stiff window had finally opened.
She went to rinse the mugs.
There was only one.
Puzzled, she checked the table, the counter, even the hallway. Nothing. On the back of the chair where he had sat lay a single folded scrap of paper. Her fingers trembled as she opened it.
Thank you for letting me be home for a while.
No signature. Just the careful handwriting of someone who had taken their time.
She stood very still. The hallway clock ticked on. Outside, the wind settled into a calmer rhythm, and the storm drifted away across the fields.
For the first time in years, Mrs Turner turned on the porch light — not because she was expecting anyone, but because, suddenly, it felt right to leave a door open to the world.
In case another visitor ever found themselves lost.
