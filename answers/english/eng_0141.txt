Overall, Source A constructs the orchard as a location of beauty and tranquility, whereas Source B presents the orchard as a place of mere profit and income.

In source A, the orchard is described as having a magical and lively atmosphere, presented as a place of immense beauty. Through the trees "stood [...] like thoughtful old friends" the orchard is constructed as a place of comfort and peace. This suggests that the value of the orchard is not through the agricultural, numerical income but through the magnificence of its inner and outer beauty. 

In Source B, the writer constructs the orchard as a commodity and exploitative supplier of money. Through the description of it as a "productive orchard", it is merely presented as a source of income, with the only purpose of providing goods for the business. This dismisses the beauty of the nature of the orchard and instead commodifies it as something to use rather than enjoy. However, later in the extract, the orchard is presented as "not a machine, but a living system" which contrasts with this earlier idea. The writer conveys the orchard as a profitable source of income, but despite this recognises the fact that it is alive. This presents the orchard as a business which must be looked after, the same as a child should be, as it is not perfect and fixed like a machine.
