The two writers describe orchards with very different purposes and atmospheres. In Source A, the orchard is presented as a peaceful and reflective place. The writer visits at sunset and he experiences a calm and a atmosphere like magic.  In the text, the writer describes the trees as “thoughtful old friends” and feeling “a curious sense of anticipation.” The orchard becomes a space for emotional stillness and personal insight. In contrast, Source B presents the orchard as a site of practical labour. Its purpose is productivity, and the atmosphere is focused, methodical, and work‑driven. The writer talks about soil conditions, pruning, and pests, showing that the orchard is valued for efficiency rather than beauty.

The writers’ relationships with their orchards also differ. In Source A, the writer feels a deep emotional connection, returning to the orchard for “reassurance” and carrying the memory of it “like a small flame.” This shows a personal, almost spiritual bond. Meanwhile, in Source B, the writer’s relationship is professional and disciplined. They emphasise “commitment,” “vigilance,” and “understanding,” suggesting respect for the orchard but not emotional attachment. Their satisfaction comes from successful harvests rather than personal reflection.
