Both sources describe orchards, but they present them from very different perspectives, creating contrasting purposes, atmospheres, and relationships with the land.

In Source A, the orchard is presented as a personal, reflective space. The purpose of the visit is not work but emotional and sensory experience. The atmosphere is calm, intimate, and almost magical, created through imagery such as apples glowing “golden-red” and the orchard seeming to “breathe”. The writer’s relationship with the orchard is emotional and personal; it becomes a place of memory, joy, and reassurance. The orchard offers comfort and reflection, and the writer returns to it repeatedly to reconnect with these feelings.

In contrast, Source B presents the orchard as a working environment. Its purpose is practical and productive: to grow healthy fruit through careful planning and labour. The atmosphere is methodical and instructional, focusing on soil conditions, pruning, pest control, and weather patterns. There is little emotional language; instead, the tone is factual and authoritative. The writer’s relationship with the orchard is professional and managerial. The orchard is treated as a living system that must be understood, maintained, and respected through patience and discipline, rather than a place for personal reflection.

Overall, Source A portrays the orchard as a place of meaning and emotional connection, while Source B presents it as a site of responsibility, effort, and long-term commitment. The first writer engages with the orchard as a source of personal joy, whereas the second engages with it as a steward focused on care, productivity, and results.
