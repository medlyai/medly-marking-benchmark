Both sources present orchards in contrasting ways by focusing on different purposes and creating very different atmospheres. In Source A, the orchard is experienced as a place of reflection and emotional connection. The writer describes it at dusk, creating a calm and almost spiritual atmosphere in which the orchard feels alive and comforting. The purpose of the visit is not practical but personal, as the narrator seeks stillness and reassurance, finding “unexpected joy” simply through being present in the moment.

In contrast, Source B presents the orchard as a site of labour and productivity. The atmosphere is practical and controlled, with the writer focusing on soil conditions, pruning, and pest management. Rather than calm or emotional, the tone is instructional and measured, emphasising discipline and responsibility. The orchard’s purpose is to produce a successful harvest, and its value is judged by yield and long-term maintenance rather than personal experience.

The writers’ relationships with the orchards also differ significantly. In Source A, the writer’s relationship is intimate and reflective; the orchard is personified and treated almost as a companion that offers comfort and perspective. The narrator returns for reassurance, suggesting an emotional bond built over time. However, in Source B, the relationship is professional and observational. The orchardist views the trees as a living system to be managed through knowledge and patience, showing respect but not emotional attachment. The connection is based on understanding and stewardship rather than personal fulfilment.

Overall, Source A presents the orchard as a place of emotional meaning and quiet joy, while Source B presents it as a working environment shaped by careful planning and sustained effort.
