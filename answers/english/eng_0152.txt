The writer uses rich, sensory and figurative language to present the orchard at dusk as peaceful, vivid, and emotionally significant.

Firstly, imagery and colour are used to capture the changing light at dusk. The apples are described as “deep golden-red” and the sky as “crimson,” “burnt orange,” and “pale lavender.” This gradual shift in colour mirrors the slow movement from day to night and creates a warm, almost glowing atmosphere. The writer’s suggestion that each apple “carried a small piece of the sun within it” uses metaphor to suggest lasting warmth and life, even as the light fades.
The writer also uses personification to make the orchard feel alive and responsive. The trees are described as “thoughtful old friends,” which suggests familiarity and comfort, while the orchard itself appears to be “waiting” and later “breathe[s] along with me.” This creates the sense that the orchard is a living companion rather than a passive setting, deepening the emotional connection between the writer and the place.

In addition, sensory language appeals to touch, smell, and taste to immerse the reader in the scene. The “rough and cold” bark grounds the writer physically, while the “smell of crisp leaves and damp soil” reinforces the freshness of dusk. The taste of the apple is described as “sharp and vivid,” emphasising how real and intense the moment feels compared to everyday experiences.

Finally, simile and metaphor are used to reflect the slowing of time at dusk. Time is said to stretch “like honey dripping from a spoon,” suggesting calm, thickness, and unhurried movement. This reinforces the tranquil atmosphere and shows how dusk allows the writer to pause and reflect.

Overall, the writer’s use of vivid imagery, personification, and sensory detail transforms the orchard at dusk into a peaceful, living space that encourages reflection and emotional awareness.
