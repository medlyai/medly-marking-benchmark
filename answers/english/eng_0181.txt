Both Source A and Source B present orchards, but the writers convey very different perspectives through contrasting methods, tones and language choices. Source A presents the orchard as a place of emotional reflection and personal meaning, while Source B presents it as a working environment shaped by discipline, knowledge and responsibility.

In Source A, the writer’s perspective is deeply personal and reflective. This is conveyed through first-person narration and emotive language, which invites the reader into the writer’s thoughts and feelings. The orchard is described using vivid imagery, such as apples that “glowed a deep golden-red”, suggesting warmth and beauty. Personification is also used frequently; the orchard seems to be “waiting” and later appears to “breathe” alongside the writer. This presents the orchard as alive and responsive, reflecting the writer’s emotional connection to it. The perspective is that of someone seeking calm and reassurance, rather than utility.

By contrast, Source B conveys a practical and instructional perspective. The writer uses a formal tone and impersonal language, often addressing the reader indirectly through phrases such as “one must begin” and “it must be remembered”. This creates distance and authority, suggesting expertise rather than emotional involvement. The orchard is viewed as a system that requires management, shown through technical vocabulary like “well-drained soil”, “pruning”, and “vigilance against pests”. These choices emphasise control and responsibility, reinforcing the perspective of the orchard as a place of labour.

The writers also differ in how they structure their accounts to convey perspective. Source A follows a narrative structure, moving through a single evening from sunset to dusk. This gradual shift in light mirrors the writer’s internal journey towards peace and joy, particularly when they experience “a sudden, unexpected joy”. The orchard becomes a symbol of escape from a rushed life, reinforced by metaphors such as time “stretching and slowing, like honey dripping from a spoon”.

In contrast, Source B is structured logically and sequentially, reflecting its practical purpose. Each paragraph focuses on a specific aspect of orchard management, such as soil, tree selection, pruning, pests, and weather. This organised structure mirrors the writer’s belief that success depends on careful planning and long-term observation. Even when the writer acknowledges that an orchard is “not a machine but a living system”, the emphasis remains on understanding and discipline rather than emotional connection.

Furthermore, the writers’ attitudes to time reveal their differing perspectives. In Source A, time slows and becomes meaningful; the moment is fleeting yet precious. The writer returns each year for reassurance, showing a lasting emotional bond. In Source B, time is measured in seasons and years, with “records kept over many seasons” proving valuable. This highlights a long-term, outcome-focused relationship rather than one based on memory or feeling.

Overall, Source A conveys a perspective of the orchard as a sanctuary and source of emotional renewal, using imagery, personification and reflective narration. Source B presents the orchard as a place of skilled labour and careful stewardship, conveyed through formal tone, technical language and structured explanation. The writers’ different methods clearly reflect their contrasting purposes and relationships with the orchard.
