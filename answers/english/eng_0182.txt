Both writers present orchards from very different perspectives, which they convey through contrasting purposes, tones, and language choices.

In Source A, the writer views the orchard as a place of personal reflection and emotional significance. This perspective is conveyed through descriptive, sensory language and figurative methods. The orchard is personified as “waiting” and later described as breathing “along with me,” suggesting a shared, almost intimate relationship between the writer and the landscape. The focus on colour imagery, such as “crimson,” “burnt orange,” and “pale lavender,” reflects the writer’s emotional response to the changing light at dusk rather than any practical concern. 

Structural choices also support this perspective: the writer moves from a specific moment in the past to reflections that continue “in the years since,” showing how the orchard has lasting meaning beyond a single visit.
By contrast, Source B presents the orchard from a practical and professional perspective, treating it as a working system that must be carefully managed. This is conveyed through a formal, instructional tone and the use of technical vocabulary such as “well-drained soil,” “pruning,” and “vigilance against pests.” Instead of emotional imagery, the writer relies on logical sequencing and cause-and-effect explanations, for example explaining how neglected pruning “encourages disease and limits fruit production.” This method reinforces the idea that the orchard’s value lies in productivity and long-term results rather than personal experience.

The writers also differ in how they present time and reward. 
In Source A, time slows and becomes meaningful, shown through the simile “like honey dripping from a spoon,” emphasising calm and mindfulness. In Source B, time is presented as something that must be tracked and learned from, with “records kept over many seasons” valued for improving future harvests. This contrast highlights Source A’s reflective viewpoint against Source B’s measured, disciplined approach.

Overall, Source A conveys the orchard as a source of joy and reassurance through evocative imagery and personification, while Source B presents it as a living but manageable system through factual detail, structured argument, and technical language. The different methods used in each source clearly reflect the writers’ contrasting perspectives.
