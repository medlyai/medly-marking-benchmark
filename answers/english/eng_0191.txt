Both writers describe an orchard, but they convey very different perspectives through their tone, language and focus. Source A presents the orchard as a place of emotional meaning and personal reflection, while Source B presents it as a working environment that must be managed with care and skill.

In Source A, the orchard is shown as a sensory and almost magical space. The writer uses figurative language and imagery to express their emotional response to the place. For example, the apples “glowed a deep golden-red” as if they carried “a small piece of the sun”, which suggests warmth and wonder. The orchard is also personified when it is described as “waiting” and later as “breathing”, making it feel alive and connected to the writer. This conveys a perspective that the orchard is not just a physical place but something that offers comfort and meaning. The writer’s feelings of “anticipation” and “unexpected joy” show that their experience is deeply personal.

In contrast, Source B presents the orchard from a practical and professional perspective. The language is more technical and factual, focusing on “soil”, “pruning”, “pests” and “weather”. This shows that the orchardist sees the orchard as something that must be carefully controlled and maintained in order to succeed. Unlike Source A, there is little emotional language; instead, the writer warns that “neglect… invites ruin”, which creates a serious and disciplined tone. This perspective suggests that the orchard is valuable because of what it produces, not because of how it makes someone feel.

The writers also differ in how they show their relationship with the orchard. In Source A, the writer returns each year because the orchard gives them reassurance and peace. They describe carrying its warmth “like a small flame”, which shows a lasting emotional connection. However, in Source B, the orchardist’s relationship is based on responsibility and patience. Phrases like “steady commitment” and “records kept over many seasons” suggest a long-term, disciplined approach rather than a sentimental one.

Overall, Source A uses poetic language, imagery and emotion to convey a reflective and personal view of the orchard, while Source B uses precise, instructional language to present the orchard as a system that must be understood and controlled. One writer experiences the orchard with the heart, while the other views it through work and knowledge.
