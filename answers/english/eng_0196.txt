The writers of Source A and Source B present contrasting perspectives on the orchard, shaped by their different purposes and experiences. In Source A, the orchard is a place of emotional significance and personal reflection. The writer describes it with poetic, sensory language, focusing on light, colour, and atmosphere. This creates a sense of wonder and tranquillity, suggesting that the orchard offers escape, stillness, and joy.

In contrast, the writer of Source B views the orchard through a practical and instructional lens. Their perspective is grounded in expertise and responsibility. They emphasise soil conditions, drainage, tree varieties, and pruning, using factual, precise language. This conveys the orchard as a site of labour, planning, and long‑term commitment rather than emotional retreat.

The methods used also differ. Source A relies on imagery, metaphor, and personification to evoke mood and emotion, such as describing trees as “thoughtful old friends.” Source B uses formal vocabulary and a logical structure, presenting information step by step to guide the reader. While Source A highlights personal connection and beauty, Source B highlights knowledge and discipline.

Together, the texts show how the same setting can inspire very different perspectives depending on purpose and relationship.
