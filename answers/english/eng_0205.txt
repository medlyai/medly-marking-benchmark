Both Source A and Source B describe orchards, but they convey very different perspectives through their purpose, tone, and language choices. Source A presents the orchard as a place of personal reflection and emotional significance, while Source B views the orchard as a working environment that requires discipline, knowledge, and long-term care.

In Source A, the writer’s perspective is intimate and subjective, focusing on personal experience rather than practical function. The orchard is described at dusk using rich sensory imagery, such as the apples that “glowed a deep golden-red” and the sky filled with “crimson clouds” and “pale lavender”. This creates a calm, almost dreamlike atmosphere, suggesting the orchard is a space for reflection and emotional connection. The writer’s relationship with the orchard is deeply personal; they describe feeling “a sudden, unexpected joy” and returning to the place each autumn for reassurance. This suggests the orchard holds symbolic meaning, representing stillness, memory, and continuity in contrast to the busyness of everyday life.

By contrast, Source B conveys a practical and instructional perspective, presenting the orchard as something to be managed and understood. The language is factual and authoritative, with technical vocabulary such as “well-drained soil”, “pruning”, and “vigilance against pests”. This establishes the writer as an expert orchard-keeper, whose relationship with the orchard is based on responsibility rather than emotion. Unlike Source A, there is little sensory description; instead, the focus is on cause and effect, for example how “branches cross and crowd one another” if pruning is neglected. This reflects a perspective that values productivity, planning, and control.

The methods used by each writer further reinforce their differing viewpoints. Source A uses metaphor and personification, such as describing the trees as “thoughtful old friends” and suggesting the orchard was “waiting”. This humanises the setting and emphasises emotional connection. In contrast, Source B relies on logical sequencing and declarative statements, guiding the reader through the processes of orchard labour. Sentences are often longer and explanatory, reinforcing its informative purpose and steady, measured tone.

Overall, Source A portrays the orchard as a place of quiet meaning and personal renewal, where the writer is a reflective visitor. Source B presents the orchard as a living system requiring care and discipline, where the writer is a knowledgeable caretaker. Through contrasting language, tone, and structure, both writers successfully convey their distinct perspectives on what an orchard represents.
