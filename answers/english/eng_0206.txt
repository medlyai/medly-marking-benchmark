In source A, the writer's perspective on the orchard is shown to be about appreciation whereas in source B, the writer's perspective on the orchard is more about caring for it. In source A, "The orchard became the whole of" the writer's "world, complete and self-contained". The use of a hyperbole suggests that his life is all about the orchard and is the reason as to why it is "complete and self contained". This creates an idea that if there are no orchards, there is nothing else to live for. However in source B, the writer uses more technical terms such as "pruning" and "harvest" to show the "diligent orchardist" treating the trees as a way to make a living. This symbolises two different types of love for the trees where one is for personal comfort and the other is for financial stability. 
On the other hand, both source A and B share the same perspective to display the orchards as a source of knowledge and guidance. In source A, the writer is "reminded that joy does not always announce itself loudly". The use of personification implies that joy is a subtle thing that doesn't go noticed but has such a large impact on our lives. This as a result creates the idea that trees are an epitome of how to live life. Similarly, in source B the "orchardist" learns that an orchard "teaches attention, restraint, and respect for natural limits". The use of a tricolon refers to the morals that are valued by everyone in life, including trees. This suggests that the tree is perhaps speaking from experience and is trying to nurture its caretaker as a return, just like in source A.
