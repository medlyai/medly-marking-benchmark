Dear Minister for Education,

I am writing to express my views on the claim that teenagers are fundamentally different from adults in how they learn, socialise, and sleep, and that education therefore requires a radical re-think. I strongly agree with this statement. The current education system too often expects teenagers to function like smaller versions of adults, when scientific evidence and lived experience show this is simply not the case.

Firstly, teenagers’ biological sleep patterns are widely recognised as being different from those of adults. Scientific research has shown that adolescents naturally fall asleep later and wake later due to changes in their circadian rhythm. Yet many schools begin lessons before 9 a.m., forcing students to learn while exhausted. This lack of sleep does not build resilience; it damages concentration, memory, and mental health. Expecting a sleep-deprived teenager to perform well academically is like asking someone to run a race with weights tied to their ankles. If education truly values achievement, it must align school schedules with how students’ bodies actually function.

Furthermore, teenagers learn differently from adults. Young people benefit far more from interactive, discussion-based learning than from passive listening. However, many classrooms still rely heavily on long explanations, rigid routines, and high-pressure testing. While exams have their place, an over-reliance on memorisation discourages curiosity and creativity. Teenagers are naturally inquisitive, yet the current system often trains them to fear mistakes rather than learn from them. Education should nurture developing minds, not reduce learning to a checklist of content to be recalled under stress.

In addition, social development is a crucial part of adolescence. Teenagers learn through collaboration, debate, and shared experience. However, schools frequently treat socialising as a distraction rather than a valuable learning tool. Group work, project-based learning, and structured discussion not only improve understanding but also develop communication skills essential for adult life. Ironically, while schools aim to prepare students for the “real world”, they often restrict the very skills that world demands: teamwork, adaptability, and problem-solving.

Critics may argue that a radical re-think would lower standards or make education too lenient. However, adapting education to meet students’ needs does not mean abandoning discipline or ambition. On the contrary, students who feel rested, understood, and engaged are more likely to be motivated and successful. High expectations should remain, but they must be supported by systems that allow students to meet them.

In conclusion, teenagers are not adults, and pretending otherwise benefits no one. An education system designed around outdated assumptions risks failing an entire generation. By rethinking school start times, teaching methods, and attitudes towards social learning, we can create an education system that works with students rather than against them. I urge you to consider these changes carefully, not as a concession, but as an investment in the future of our society.

Yours sincerely,
A concerned student
