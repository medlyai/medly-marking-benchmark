Dear Minister,

I am writing to share my views on the statement that teenagers are not like adults, and that education should change to meet their needs. I believe this is largely true, and schools could improve if they took teenage development more seriously.

Teenagers learn and socialise differently from adults, yet they are often treated the same way. For example, many schools expect students to sit still and concentrate for long periods, which can be difficult for teenagers. They often learn better through discussion, group work, or active lessons. If lessons were more varied, students might feel more engaged and motivated to learn.

Sleep is another important issue. Scientists say teenagers need more sleep and tend to feel tired later at night. Yet most schools start very early, so many students arrive exhausted. This makes it harder for them to concentrate and perform well. Starting the school day slightly later could help students feel more alert and ready to learn.

I do not think education needs a complete radical re-think. Schools still play an important role in preparing young people for adult life, teaching responsibility and discipline. Deadlines, exams, and rules help students understand expectations and prepare for the future. Instead of changing everything, schools could make sensible adjustments that recognise how teenagers develop.

In conclusion, I believe the education system should be more flexible and considerate of teenage needs, particularly regarding learning styles and sleep. Small changes could make a big difference to students’ wellbeing and success.
