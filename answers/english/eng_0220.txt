Dear Minister for Education,

At 8:30 on a dark Monday morning, I sit in a classroom staring at the board, eyes heavy and mind foggy. Around me, chairs creak, students yawn and teachers repeat instructions that few of us are fully awake to understand. This daily scene is not caused by laziness or lack of ambition; it is the result of an education system that fails to recognise a simple truth — teenagers are not adults.

Scientific research shows that adolescents learn, socialise and even sleep differently from grown-ups, yet schools continue to operate as though students are miniature adults who can function perfectly on early starts and rigid routines. Teenagers’ brains are still developing, meaning we respond better to active, engaging lessons than long periods of passive listening. However, many classrooms remain dominated by silence, note-taking and exam pressure. This outdated approach limits creativity and discourages students who might otherwise thrive.

Sleep is another crucial factor that education policy consistently ignores. Teenagers’ natural body clocks make it difficult to fall asleep early, yet schools insist on early start times that leave students exhausted before lessons begin. As a result, concentration drops, behaviour worsens and mental health suffers. Is it reasonable to expect high achievement from students who are permanently sleep-deprived? A later start to the school day would not lower standards; instead, it would raise them by allowing students to learn when they are most alert.

Moreover, education should develop confident, capable individuals, not just successful exam candidates. Adolescence is a key time for learning social skills, teamwork and independence. Unfortunately, the current system prioritises grades above all else, turning learning into a constant cycle of stress and revision. While exams are important, they should not overshadow opportunities for discussion, collaboration and real-world problem-solving — skills that matter long after school ends.

In conclusion, teenagers should not be forced into an education system designed without them in mind. By adapting school hours, teaching methods and definitions of success, education can better support both achievement and wellbeing. If meaningful change is not made, tomorrow morning will look exactly like today: another tired student in another classroom, struggling to stay awake at 8:30, wondering why education refuses to wake up.

Yours sincerely,
A concerned student
