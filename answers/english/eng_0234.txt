Dear Minister,

I am writing to express my views on the increasingly urgent discussion surrounding the needs of teenage students. Scientists argue that teenagers are fundamentally different from adults in how they learn, socialise, and sleep. I believe this statement deserves serious attention, and that our education system must adapt if it is to serve young people effectively.

To begin with, the scientific evidence on teenage sleep patterns is compelling. Teenagers experience a natural shift in their body clocks, meaning they fall asleep later and wake later. Yet we continue to insist on early school start times that directly contradict their biology. The result is predictable: tired students, reduced concentration, and classrooms full of young people who are physically present but mentally exhausted. If we want students to learn well, we must allow them to be awake enough to do so.

Furthermore, teenagers do not learn in the same way adults do. Their brains are still developing, particularly in areas linked to decision‑making, emotional regulation, and long‑term planning. This does not mean they are incapable; rather, it means they need teaching that recognises their stage of development. More interactive lessons, opportunities for collaboration, and time to reflect would support the way they naturally process information. Instead, many students face a rigid, exam‑driven system that values memorisation over understanding.

Social development is another area where change is needed. Teenagers learn a great deal from interacting with their peers, yet the current system often restricts meaningful social time to short breaks or hurried lunchtimes. If we want confident, well‑rounded young adults, we must give them space to build relationships, work together, and develop the social skills that employers consistently say are lacking.

Some may argue that altering the system would be disruptive or costly. However, the cost of not changing is far greater. We risk creating a generation of students who are disengaged, stressed, and underperforming—not because they lack ability, but because the system refuses to meet them where they are. A radical re‑think does not mean abandoning structure; it means designing a structure that works.

In practical terms, later start times, more flexible timetables, and teaching methods that reflect adolescent development would be a powerful start. These changes would not only improve academic outcomes but also support students’ mental health and overall wellbeing.

Minister, our education system should empower young people, not exhaust them. If we truly want students to thrive, we must listen to the science and reshape education to reflect the realities of teenage life. I urge you to consider these changes seriously and to lead the way in creating a system that understands and supports the students it serves.

Yours sincerely,
