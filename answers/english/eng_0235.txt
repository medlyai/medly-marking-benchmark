Dear Minister for Education,

I am writing to express my views on the claim that education needs a radical re-think in order to meet the needs of teenagers, who scientists say learn, socialise, and sleep differently from adults. I strongly believe that this statement is correct and that the current education system does not fully support how young people actually develop and learn.

Firstly, scientific research has shown that teenagers’ brains are still developing, particularly the areas responsible for concentration, emotional control, and decision-making. However, schools often expect students to behave like adults, sitting silently for long periods and coping with constant pressure from exams and assessments. This approach can be unrealistic and damaging. Teenagers often learn more effectively through active methods such as discussion, collaboration, and practical tasks, yet these are not always prioritised in classrooms.

Another major issue is sleep. Teenagers naturally fall asleep later and need more rest than adults, yet many schools start early in the morning. This results in widespread tiredness, reduced focus, and increased stress among students. Expecting teenagers to perform academically while sleep-deprived is unfair and counterproductive. A later school start time could significantly improve students’ wellbeing, behaviour, and ability to learn.

Social development is also an important factor. Teenagers learn a great deal from interacting with their peers, but education often focuses too heavily on individual achievement and exam results. While academic success is important, schools should also encourage teamwork, communication, and problem-solving skills, which are essential for adult life. These skills are best developed through group work and shared learning experiences.

That said, change must be introduced carefully. Not all students learn in the same way, and schools still require structure and consistency. Any reforms should be based on research and tested gradually to ensure they benefit all students rather than disadvantaging some.

In conclusion, teenagers are not simply adults in smaller bodies, and education should reflect this reality. By rethinking how schools approach learning, sleep, and social development, we can create a system that is healthier, fairer, and more effective. I urge you to consider reforms that place students’ needs at the centre of education.
