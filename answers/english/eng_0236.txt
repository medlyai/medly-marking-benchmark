Dear Minister for Education,

By means of introduction, I am currently a secondary school student. My studies  have allowed me to become versed in various cognitive studies related to pedagogy and adolescent development. I am writing to you regarding a study that was recently brought to my attention. 

This study stipulates that a radical rethink on education is required to better meet the needs of students, as adolescents have social and learning needs that differ from adults.  These differences can also result in different sleeping patterns.  As a member of the adolescent community, I wanted to provide you with my perspective on this topic. I feel it is imperative that feedback is taken on board from all stakeholders of this study and not just those considered “experts,” to ensure that a rounded assessment of any next steps can be achieved. 

It cannot be denied that teenagers are often burdened by stereotypes associated with puberty, frequently being dismissed as irrational or overly emotional. The teenage years are widely recognised as one of the most turbulent periods in a person’s life, a claim supported by numerous scientific studies into adolescent cognitive development. 

Alongside these biological changes, adolescents are simultaneously expected to navigate significant academic pressures, such as GCSE examinations and decisions that may shape their future careers. Hormonal imbalance during puberty can contribute to fatigue and heightened emotional sensitivity, which must be managed alongside increasing levels of stress and anxiety. Expecting students to perform at an adult standard while undergoing such changes is not only unrealistic, but potentially will be to the detriment  of the desired outcome.

In order to optimally navigate this issue, I have undertaken studies and interviews with my peers to understand how you can best serve us, so that we can best serve you in the future. An overwhelming majority of those interviewed stated that average school start times of 8am are too early and unnecessary. A slightly later start time of 9am would not only be more practical but also mirror the model that is currently in place for those in full time employment, therefore better preparing us for the world of adulthood when we complete our education.

 In addition many felt that there was too much burden placed upon them via homework or coursework. Whilst it was  agreed homework is vital for success, it should not interfere with our “work-life balance” a topic which is constantly reiterated as being important for adults, but not so much for adolescents. Here a simple solution could be to incorporate more “free” or “study” periods within the school timetable. This is something that happens for A level students with great success. 

In conclusion, it is clear that the current education system does not fully reflect the biological, emotional, and social realities of adolescence. By recognising that teenagers learn, socialise, and rest differently from adults, meaningful changes can be made that benefit both students and society as a whole. Adjustments such as later start times and more balanced workloads would not lower standards, but rather allow students to meet them more effectively. I urge you to consider the voices of young people alongside scientific research when shaping future educational policy, as investing in students’ wellbeing today will result in a more capable, motivated, and resilient workforce.
